## The three seed sources

1. **Economics Network — Health Economics for Teachers** — https://economicsnetwork.ac.uk/health/teachers
   A 14-module university teaching curriculum (lecture notes, readings, exercises) that this book's chapter list is built on. Each module has a sub-page at `/health/<slug>`: intro, evaluation, modelling, policy, systems, demand, market_failure, rationing, equity, trade, behavioural, pharma, capabilities, econometrics. Per-module detail is below.

2. **Wikipedia — Health economics** — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_economics
   Discipline overview. Key content: Arrow's "Uncertainty and the Welfare Economics of Medical Care" (1963) as the founding paper; Alan Williams' eight-area "plumbing diagram" (what influences health; what is health and its value; demand for healthcare; supply of healthcare; micro-economic evaluation at treatment level; market equilibrium; whole-system evaluation; planning/budgeting/monitoring); the Grossman model (1972, health as depreciating capital stock, consumption + investment good); market failures (adverse selection, moral hazard — RAND Health Insurance Experiment, information asymmetry, supplier-induced demand, Baumol effect); evaluation methods (CEA, CUA, CBA, QALY, DALY, ICER; NICE and IQWiG as institutional examples); five healthcare markets (financing; physician/nursing services; institutional services; input factors; professional education); subfields (pharmacoeconomics, HTA, mental health economics, nursing economics); Ayushman Bharat (India, 2018) as the largest public coverage scheme; key journals (*Journal of Health Economics*, *Health Economics*, *Value in Health*, *PharmacoEconomics*).

3. **GOV.UK — Health economics: a guide for public health teams** — https://www.gov.uk/guidance/health-economics-a-guide-for-public-health-teams
   A national exemplar of practitioner-facing health economics (Public Health England lineage; last updated November 2025). Content in seven areas: topic-specific ROI/cost-effectiveness tools (best start in life, CVD prevention, contraception, falls, mental health, MSK, oral health, older adults, weight management, diabetes prevention); burden tools (air pollution costs, employment ROI); modelling reports (multimorbidity costs, alcohol-attributable fractions); a prioritisation framework for local authorities; the Health Economics Evidence Resources (HEER) database; spend-vs-outcome benchmarking tools (SPOT, DOVE); free e-learning modules. Useful as a worked example of how governments package health economics for non-specialists.

## Per-topic notes (from the 14 Economics Network modules)

Format per topic: concepts → key names/models → readings worth citing → what the chapter should cover. Home chapters per spec §4 manifest.

### Introduction to health economics → Chapter 1.1

- **Concepts:** scarcity, opportunity cost, efficiency, marginal analysis; demand/supply/equilibrium and consumer/producer surplus in health markets; why health care differs economically; structure of the health care industry; qualitative research's role.
- **Names:** Kenneth Arrow, Alan Williams, Selma Mushkin (early theory), Anthony Culyer.
- **Readings:** Morris, Devlin & Parkin, *Economic Analysis in Health Care*; Folland, Goodman & Stano, *The Economics of Health and Health Care*; Wonderling, Gruen & Black, *Introduction to Health Economics*; Arrow (AER 1963).
- **Chapter should cover:** scarcity and opportunity cost applied to health; what makes health care economically distinctive; the Williams plumbing diagram as the book's map; the discipline's history and institutions worldwide.

### Economic evaluation → Chapter 2.1

- **Concepts:** CEA/CUA/CBA (plus ROI/SROI from the GOV.UK source); QALYs (calculation, limitations), DALYs; valuing outcomes — EQ-5D, visual analogue scale, time trade-off, willingness to pay; costing, discounting, inflation; ICERs, thresholds (thresholds vary widely by country — roughly 0.1–1.5× GDP per capita in practice), decision rules; perspective.
- **Names:** Michael Drummond, Mark Sculpher, Nancy Devlin, Cam Donaldson; US Panel on Cost-Effectiveness in Health and Medicine.
- **Readings:** Drummond et al., *Methods for the Economic Evaluation of Health Care Programmes*; Gold, Siegel, Russell & Weinstein, *Cost-Effectiveness in Health and Medicine*; Fox-Rushby & Cairns, *Economic Evaluation*.
- **Chapter should cover:** framing the question; measuring and valuing outcomes; costing and discounting; ICERs, thresholds and decision rules across countries (NICE, IQWiG, WHO-CHOICE as contrasting institutional approaches); critiques of the QALY.

### Modelling → Chapter 2.2

- **Concepts:** decision-analytic modelling; decision trees, Markov models, microsimulation; trial-based vs model-based evaluation; sensitivity and probabilistic uncertainty analysis; modelling within HTA.
- **Names:** Briggs, Sculpher & Claxton framework.
- **Readings:** Briggs, Sculpher & Claxton, *Decision Modelling for Health Economic Evaluation*; Glick et al., *Economic Evaluation in Clinical Trials*; Gray et al., *Applied Methods of Cost-Effectiveness Analysis in Health Care*.
- **Chapter should cover:** why model (extrapolation beyond trials); model types and when each fits; uncertainty handling; validation; models inside HTA processes worldwide.

### Health Policy → Chapter 3.2

- **Concepts:** rationales and instruments of government intervention; public insurance design (Medicare/Medicaid as US examples; NHS funding as UK example); targets and performance management ("targets and terror", waiting times); patient choice and competition reforms and their equity effects; provider payment and workforce policy; fiscal/pricing levers.
- **Names:** Carol Propper, Alan Maynard, John Appleby, Karen Bloor.
- **Readings:** Olsen, *Principles in Health Economics and Policy*; McPake & Normand, *Health Economics: An International Perspective*; Mooney, *Key Issues in Health Economics*.
- **Chapter should cover:** the policy toolkit; evidence-to-policy translation; prevention economics; regulation; comparative policy case studies from several countries.

### Health Systems → Chapter 3.1

- **Concepts:** typologies of financing (tax-funded/Beveridge, social insurance/Bismarck, private insurance, out-of-pocket); third-party payer effects; public–private mix; provider payment (capitation, fee-for-service, DRGs); system reform; workforce planning; international comparison.
- **Names:** Alan Maynard, Karen Bloor; Gillette's double-oral-auction third-party-payment classroom experiment.
- **Readings:** Keen, Light & Mays, *Public–Private Relations in Health Care*; Maynard, *The Public–Private Mix for Health*; OECD *Health at a Glance*; WHO world health reports (the World Health Report identifies people's four roles: contributors, citizens, providers, consumers).
- **Chapter should cover:** system typologies with named country exemplars across income levels; how payment shapes behaviour; comparison methods and their pitfalls; sustainability.

### Demand for health and healthcare → Chapter 1.2

- **Concepts:** Grossman model (health as human capital; consumption + investment good; optimal stock declines with age); Wagstaff's empirical reformulation; derived demand; price/income elasticities of health care demand; demand for insurance; agency.
- **Names:** Michael Grossman, Adam Wagstaff.
- **Readings:** Grossman, *The Demand for Health* (NBER 1972); Wagstaff, "The demand for health: an empirical reformulation of the Grossman model" (*Health Economics* 1993); McGuire, Henderson & Mooney, *The Economics of Health Care*.
- **Chapter should cover:** health as capital; critiques/extensions of Grossman; empirical evidence on health production; elasticities; insurance demand (distortions themselves belong to 1.3).

### Market Failure → Chapter 1.3

- **Concepts:** Arrow's 1963 analysis; uncertainty and asymmetric information; agency and supplier-induced demand; adverse selection and moral hazard (RAND Health Insurance Experiment; Finkelstein's work); externalities incl. antimicrobial resistance as a commons problem; monopoly; Baumol effect; limits of market allocation.
- **Names:** Kenneth Arrow, Amy Finkelstein.
- **Readings:** Arrow, "Uncertainty and the welfare economics of medical care" (*American Economic Review* 1963); Donaldson & Gerard, *Economics of Health Care Financing: The Visible Hand*; Finkelstein, "Moral Hazard in Health Insurance" (2017 Marshall Lecture).
- **Chapter should cover:** the full catalogue of failures with health-specific mechanisms; why insurance markets unravel; externalities and public goods (vaccination, AMR); why every country intervenes (responses themselves belong to 3.2).

### Rationing → Chapter 3.3

- **Concepts:** unavoidability of rationing; explicit vs implicit rationing and the political cost of explicitness; rationing by waiting; priority-setting processes (the Oregon experiment); equity–efficiency trade-offs in allocation; procedural fairness — Daniels & Sabin's "accountability for reasonableness"; disinvestment.
- **Names:** Norman Daniels & James Sabin; Ubel; Coast.
- **Readings:** Ubel, *Pricing Life*; Coulter & Ham, *The Global Challenge of Health Care Rationing*; Daniels & Sabin, *Setting Limits Fairly*; Ham, "Health care rationing" (*BMJ* 1995).
- **Chapter should cover:** rationing mechanisms across system types (waiting lists, cost-sharing, benefit packages, HTA); Oregon as the canonical explicit-rationing case; procedural fairness; the psychology and politics of saying no.

### Equity → Chapter 3.4

- **Concepts:** horizontal/vertical equity; equity in finance vs delivery vs outcomes; measurement (Wagstaff/van Doorslaer methods, concentration curves); equity–efficiency trade-offs; distributive justice; ethics of public health.
- **Names:** Adam Wagstaff, Eddy van Doorslaer, Amartya Sen, Paul Dolan, Victor Fuchs.
- **Readings:** van Doorslaer, Wagstaff & Rutten, *Equity in the Finance and Delivery of Health Care*; Dolan & Olsen, *Distributing Health Care*; Anand, Peter & Sen, *Public Health, Ethics, and Equity*; Fuchs, *Who Shall Live?*.
- **Chapter should cover:** equity concepts and their rival definitions; measurement methods; global health inequalities evidence; policy tools; the trade-off with efficiency made explicit.

### Global health and trade → Chapter 3.6

- **Concepts:** globalisation–health channels; global public goods for health (disease surveillance, knowledge, genomics); health worker migration ("brain drain"); trade agreements and health policy space (TRIPS); pandemics as global externalities; priority-setting in low- and middle-income countries.
- **Names:** Richard Smith (LSHTM); Bettcher, Yach & Guindon.
- **Readings:** Smith, Beaglehole, Woodward & Drager (eds.), *Global Public Goods for Health* (OUP); Smith, "Global public goods and health" (*Bulletin of the WHO* 2003); Bettcher, Yach & Guindon, "Global trade and health" (*Bulletin of the WHO* 2000).
- **Chapter should cover:** trade–health linkages; global financing architecture (Global Fund, Gavi, World Bank); workforce flows; TRIPS and access to medicines; global governance.

### Behavioural economics → Chapter 4.1

- **Concepts:** biases and heuristics in health decisions (present bias, framing, defaults); nudges and choice architecture; behavioural insights for insurance and adherence; behavioural critiques of rational-choice health models; ethics of paternalism.
- **Names:** Richard Thaler & Cass Sunstein; Richard Frank; Thomas Rice.
- **Readings:** Thaler & Sunstein, *Nudge*; Frank, "Behavioral economics and health economics" (NBER WP 10881, 2004); Rice, "The behavioral economics of health and health care" (*Annual Review of Public Health* 2013).
- **Chapter should cover:** the bias catalogue applied to health; nudge design with international examples (organ-donation defaults, sugar-tax framing); incentives for healthy behaviour; limits and ethics.

### Pharmacoeconomics → Chapter 2.4

- **Concepts:** pharmaceutical market structure; R&D costs and innovation pricing (DiMasi estimates — contested, cite carefully); international price discrimination and regulation; generic and "me-too" competition; value assessment and reimbursement; managed entry agreements.
- **Names:** Patricia Danzon; DiMasi, Hansen & Grabowski; Office of Health Economics.
- **Readings:** Danzon & Chao, "Does Regulation Drive out Competition in Pharmaceutical Markets?" (*Journal of Law & Economics* 2000); DiMasi et al., "The Price of Innovation" (*Journal of Health Economics* 2003).
- **Chapter should cover:** how drug markets differ; pricing and reimbursement systems worldwide (external reference pricing, value-based pricing); HTA for medicines; generics/biosimilars; access in LMICs (cross-reference 3.6 for TRIPS).

### Capabilities → Chapter 3.5

- **Concepts:** Sen's capability approach translated to health; welfarism vs extra-welfarism; capability-based outcome measures (ICECAP-A, ICECAP-O); capabilities vs QALYs; applications in older-people, end-of-life, and public-health evaluation.
- **Names:** Amartya Sen; Joanna Coast; Brouwer, Culyer, van Exel & Rutten.
- **Readings:** Brouwer et al., "Welfarism vs. extra-welfarism" (*Journal of Health Economics* 2008); Coast, Smith & Lorgelly, "Welfarism, extra-welfarism and capability" (*Social Science & Medicine* 2008).
- **Chapter should cover:** the philosophical foundations; ICECAP instruments; when capability measures beat QALYs; practical adoption status.

### Health Econometrics → Chapter 2.3

- **Concepts:** distinctive features of health data (skewed costs, counts, censoring); categorical/limited dependent variable models; generalised linear models for costs; panel data; causal inference (the spec scope adds difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity); critical appraisal of applied studies.
- **Names:** Andrew Jones; Wooldridge.
- **Readings:** Jones, "Health Econometrics" in Culyer & Newhouse (eds.), *Handbook of Health Economics*; Wooldridge, *Econometric Analysis of Cross Section and Panel Data*.
- **Chapter should cover:** what the methods are *for* (this is a leader's guide, not a stats text — spec §15); when to trust a causal claim; commissioning and appraising econometric work; classic pitfalls (selection, confounding, missing data).
