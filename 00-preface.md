# Preface

**This is a working handbook for the people who decide how health money is spent — written so that any one chapter can be read on its own, the night before the decision.**

## Who this book is for

Health economics has a reputation for being the property of specialists: a discipline of Greek letters, discount rates, and quality-adjusted life years that happens somewhere off to the side of the real work. This book rejects that. Every serious decision in health and care — what to fund, what to stop, how to pay providers, whether a new technology earns its place, who waits and who does not — is an economic decision, whether or not an economist is in the room. The people who make those decisions deserve the frameworks that make them well.

So this book is written for chief executives and senior leaders; directors of finance, strategy, digital, and transformation; commissioners and payers; clinical and care professionals who sit on the committees that say yes and no; and the operational managers who turn budgets into services. It assumes intelligence, not prior training in economics. Where a technical idea earns its place, it is explained in plain language and shown doing real work.

## What it covers, and where it comes from

The book has twenty-four chapters in four parts. **Part 1 — Foundations** explains why health is economically strange: why markets that work for groceries misfire for healthcare, and the models of how people demand health and care and how it is supplied. **Part 2 — Evaluation and Evidence** is the analyst's toolkit: how to value outcomes, build models, test causal claims, assess medicines, and judge affordability. **Part 3 — Systems, Policy and Priorities** covers how societies organise, fund, ration, and share out care — including the workforce that delivers it, the insurance that pays for it, long-term and social care, and mental health, and how equity and human capability enter the account. **Part 4 — Frontiers** looks at where the discipline is moving: behavioural economics, the economics of innovation, the fast-changing economics of digital and artificial-intelligence-enabled care, and the economics of health on a warming planet.

The chapter list is built on the health economics curriculum taught in universities — the fourteen topics of the [Economics Network](https://economicsnetwork.ac.uk/health/teachers)'s teaching collection — extended with three frontier chapters the classic syllabus predates. The intellectual spine is the discipline's own: [Kenneth Arrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_Arrow)'s founding insight that medical care is not an ordinary commodity, [Michael Grossman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Grossman_(economist))'s model of health as capital, and [Alan Williams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Williams_(economist))'s map of the whole field.

## Its point of view

The book is **worldwide**. Health systems differ profoundly — tax-funded, social-insurance, private, and mixed; rich and poor — and no single country's arrangements are treated as the default. Named systems appear throughout as *exemplars of patterns*, not as the way things must be: England's National Institute for Health and Care Excellence when the subject is thresholds, Germany when it is social insurance, the United States when it is private markets and their failures, India and the World Health Organization when it is coverage at scale. Readers are expected to map the principles onto their own jurisdiction.

Its temperament is practical and honest. Health economics is, at bottom, the study of trade-offs under scarcity: every pound or rupee or dollar spent once cannot be spent again, and a sincere "yes" to one patient is a quiet "no" to another. The book does not pretend those choices away, and it does not sell certainty it cannot deliver. Where the evidence is strong, it says so; where a number would be invented, it describes the pattern instead.

## How to read it

You do not have to read it in order. Each chapter stands alone, opens with a single-sentence thesis, and follows the same shape: why the topic matters, the core concepts, numbered best practices, three questions to take to your team, a worked example, three sector lenses (start-up, enterprise, government), common failure modes, a maturity model, and a checklist. If you are new to the field, Part 1 is the natural start. If you have come for a specific decision — an evaluation to commission, a rationing policy to defend, a digital tool to appraise — go straight to that chapter and follow its cross-references outward.

## How it was built, and its limits

This handbook was assembled from public, verifiable sources, and it holds itself to a rule stated plainly in its own specification: **no invented citations, statistics, or quotations**. Where a figure could not be verified, the text describes the pattern qualitatively rather than guess. The worked examples are explicitly fictional, drawn to be realistic teaching cases, not reports of real organisations.

It is a guide to *how to think and decide economically* — not a clinical guideline, not a statistics textbook, not legal or regulatory authority, and not an encyclopaedia of every country's system. For binding decisions, consult current statute, your regulator's guidance, and your own governance. Used for what it is, it should help you make the choices in front of you more clearly, more fairly, and with your reasons open to view.
